# Transcript Intelligence — Take-Home Assignment

## Background

You're joining a team at a B2B enterprise SaaS company. The company captures call transcripts from across the organization — customer support calls (customers reaching out with issues), external calls (account managers speaking with customers about renewals, adoption, and feedback), and internal calls (engineering syncs, cross-team escalations, planning discussions).

The team is building a product called **Transcript Intelligence** — a tool that helps different stakeholders across the company make better decisions using these transcripts. Think support leaders, sales managers, product managers, engineering leads — each would want something different from this tool.

You'll receive approximately 100 sample transcripts across the three call types as your starting dataset.

Your job is to explore the dataset, extract meaningful insights, and show us how you think.

---

## What We'd Like You to Do

1. **Build a pipeline that processes the transcripts and categorizes them by topic or theme.** Show us the categories you identified, explain why you chose your approach (LLM-based, clustering, rule-based, hybrid — your call), and give examples of transcripts that fall into each category. We want to see your reasoning, not just the output.

2. **Generate a sentiment analysis across call types and identify trends.** Don't just produce charts — tell us what the trends mean. If something stands out, say what it could indicate and why someone should care.

3. **What else can you see?** Beyond the two tasks above, we'd love to see what other insights you think could be generated from this data. Think about the different stakeholders who might use a tool like this — what would be valuable to them that isn't obvious at first glance? Document at least 2–3 additional insight ideas. You can implement them or simply describe them with your reasoning for why they'd matter.

---

## How to Present Your Work

Submit three deliverables:

1. **A slide deck** — this is what you'll present. Structure it as if you're sharing findings with a product and engineering leadership team. Lead with insights, not code. Tell us what you found, why it matters, and what you'd recommend.

2. **A notebook or code repository** — this is your reference material. It should be well-organized and readable so we can review your technical approach. Include comments or markdown that explain your key decisions.

3. **A short video demo (5–10 minutes)** — Record a walkthrough of your solution in action. Show the pipeline running, walk through the outputs, and demo any insights or visualizations you've built. A simple screen recording with narration is fine — no need for professional production.

You'll have **30 minutes** to present your slide deck to a panel, followed by **15 minutes of Q&A**. During Q&A we may ask you to walk us through specific parts of your code, so be ready to pull up your notebook.

---

## A Few Ground Rules

- You're welcome to use any tools including LLMs. We're evaluating your thinking, judgment, and the quality of your insights — not whether you wrote every line from scratch.
- There's no prescribed tech stack. Use whatever you're comfortable with.
- There's no strict time limit. We've scoped this so the required tasks are achievable in a few hours. How much further you go is up to you.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I modify or augment the sample dataset?**
Absolutely. The dataset is a starting point. If you want to add synthetic data, restructure it, enrich it with additional fields, or generate more transcripts to better demonstrate your approach — go for it. Just explain what you changed and why.

**What if the dataset doesn't have all three call types (support, external, internal)?**
The sample dataset may not perfectly map to all three categories. You're welcome to relabel, reclassify, or generate additional data to fit the scenario. How you handle this is part of what we're evaluating.

**Do I need to build a production-ready system?**
No. We're not expecting deployment-ready code. We want to see your thinking, your approach, and the quality of your insights. Clean, readable code matters more than over-engineering.

**Can I use LLMs or AI tools?**
Yes — any and all tools are welcome. Copilot, ChatGPT, Claude, open-source models, whatever helps you. We care about what you do with the output, not how you got there.

**Can I generate additional data?**
Yes. If you feel you need more transcripts to validate your approach, demonstrate a pattern, or test at scale, you're welcome to generate synthetic data using LLMs or any other method. Just be transparent about what's original and what you generated, and explain why you needed more.

**What language or framework should I use?**
Your choice. Python, R, TypeScript, Go — whatever you're most productive in. Same for libraries, frameworks, and cloud services — AWS, GCP, Azure, or any other platform. Use whatever tools help you do your best work.

**How long should my slide deck be?**
However many slides you need to fill 30 minutes effectively. Don't pad it — if you can tell the story in fewer slides, that's better than stretching to fill time.

**Should I focus more on the required tasks or the bonus insights?**
The required tasks are your foundation — make sure those are solid. But the bonus insights are where you stand out. A strong required section with two or three thoughtful bonus ideas beats a perfect required section with nothing beyond it.

**What if I have a great idea but don't have time to implement it?**
Write it up. Describe what the insight is, why it matters, who would use it, and how you'd approach building it. A well-articulated idea you didn't code is more valuable to us than a half-baked implementation.

**Who will I be presenting to?**
A panel of product and engineering leaders. Pitch your findings to that audience — they care about impact and decisions, not your import statements.

**Can I ask clarifying questions before starting?**
Yes, reach out to the recruiter. But part of the exercise is seeing how you handle ambiguity, so we may not answer everything.
