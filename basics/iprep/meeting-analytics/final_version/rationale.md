# Analysis Rationale — How We Chose What to Analyze

Use this as speaker notes for the slide deck and as a prepared answer for Q&A.

---

## The Core Argument

We started with the audience, not the data.

Before writing a single query, we asked what decision each stakeholder needs to make — not what the dataset contains. A CTO doesn't need a chart of technical issues grouped by theme. They need to know which product is on fire and whether it's costing them renewals. That's a different question, and it leads to a different analysis.

---

## The Method (Top-Down)

1. **Identify who's in the room** — engineering and product leadership panel
2. **For each stakeholder, ask:** what would keep them up at night? What do they need to act on Monday morning?
3. **Derive the question from the decision**, not from the available columns
4. **Then check whether the data can answer it**

This is the opposite of the typical approach (explore data → find patterns → label them as insights). Starting from decisions means every chart has a natural "so what."

---

## Why It Produces Better Charts

When you start from a decision, the visualization is actionable by construction.

| Starting point | Chart produced | Actionable? |
|----------------|---------------|-------------|
| "What moment types exist in the data?" | Distribution of 8 moment types | No — tells you nothing to do |
| "Which product is generating the most customer-visible failures?" | Technical issues per product, ranked | Yes — tells engineering where to invest |
| "Which accounts need a call this week?" | Named watchlist with churn signals + sentiment | Yes — tells CSMs who to call |

The framing determines whether a visualization is actionable or merely descriptive.

---

## Why We Show Both Risk and Opportunity

Every stakeholder section has both risk questions and positive signal questions. This is deliberate.

- **Reliability / Detect** is the biggest risk — churn signals, negative sentiment, outage contaminating renewals
- **Comply / Comply v2** is the biggest opportunity — highest sentiment in the dataset, customers in growth mode

Showing both in the same breath signals that we're not pattern-matching on negativity — we're building a balanced picture that leadership can use to allocate resources. Pure risk analysis leads to reactive decisions. Risk + opportunity analysis leads to strategy.

---

## One-Liner for Q&A

> "We treated this as a product analytics problem, not a data exploration problem — start with decisions, work backwards to data."

---

## If Asked About the Questions Specifically

The questions weren't generated from the data. They were generated by imagining the stakeholder's job:

- A **CTO** is accountable for reliability and engineering investment. Their question is "which product is on fire and is it costing us renewals?" — not "what are the recurring technical problems?"
- A **CPO** is accountable for the roadmap. Their question is "what are customers willing to pay for that we don't have, and are they blocked or growing?" — not "what feature gaps exist?"
- A **Sales / CS leader** is accountable for revenue retention. Their question is "who needs a call today?" — not "which meetings have negative sentiment?"

The shift from description to decision is what separates a useful analytical tool from a dashboard nobody looks at.
